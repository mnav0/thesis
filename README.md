# Abstract

In our current digital age, new media increasingly allows us to experience museum collections online, removing both barriers to entry and allowing for different methods and paths of connecting with art, artists, and extracting meaning. With this ever-growing availability of publicly curated art data, this paper recenters the narrative around the people who create these works. Looking at art as a practice of self-expression, we examine what themes artists explore within their work according to both the context set by museum cataloguing practices and the artists themselves, along with the ways that they express these subjects through formal elements like color, form, and composition. Computational analysis is applied to data from open archives from the most visited art institutions in the U. S. alongside public artists’ statements and interviews to facilitate this research. The sample dataset anchors largely around abstract visual art, which can offer a dense entry point into artists’ self-expression by removing the primary goal of directly communicating a concrete depiction to the viewer. To more directly examine the process of processing topics like identity and multiplicity through creation in the present day, the sample dataset is made up of Asian-American artists since 1945 with a focus on mixed-race identifying creators. Through supplementing traditional approaches to this topic with computational and cross-disciplinary analysis, we examine the relationship between the art and the artist as well as what place technology and psychological research as executionary tools can have within archival practice and the experience of art and identity in the modern age.

# Introduction

Since 2017, many museums and cultural institutions in the United States have established open access initiatives that publish their collections and data online for public use with the goal of increasing access to art and knowledge. An increase in digital projects that utilize these archives to analyze and expose new ways of experiencing art has followed suit, allowing us to explore specific pieces of art outside of the context they may traditionally be presented in as part of an exhibit in a museum as well as collectively explore works across various collections. With these new ways to experience the data and metadata that make up an individual piece of art, where does the role of the artist fit into the archive? What information is available about the people behind the work, and how are the data and the people it describes categorized? 

Identity is rarely fully legible through one selection or field. Institutional surveys are increasingly turning to multi-select options for self-identification, especially surrounding categorical classification of race and ethnicity (United States Census Bureau, 2020). The same issue presents itself when relying on a single classification for artists, especially those with mixed and diasporic identities that are especially difficult to cleanly capture with one database field. This paper examines what types of classifications and biographical information are available in open access museum archives alongside an artist’s body of work and introduces how additional data layers may provide new readings. Through examining the relationship between the art and the artist, how can contextualization of the work in terms of its creator supplement the traditional museum viewing experience and contribute to dialogue surrounding the role of identity in archival practice? What forms of identity and self-expression are visible when reading across institutional data, artist voice, and visual practice simultaneously?

With the goal of combining different data sources to supplement existing meaning, this topic is approached by bolstering museum metadata with artist statements and interviews. In focusing the sample data on subjective narratives that are elusive to fixed meaning like abstract visual art, we can produce one of many possible interpretations. This collective data reading presents an interpretation of how artists’ navigation of identity and world experience might be represented through their work, examining patterns across multiple sources and supplementing the research with psychological theory on the multiplicity and dynamicity of identity/identity expression. Computational analysis is used to interpret this data and pull out patterns that support this research to present a new reading of publicly available information. A focus on modern and contemporary abstract practice places the research within the present day, providing a picture of how events over the last century have affected modern identity groups. 

As a test case to analyze a dynamic diasporic identity group, the sample dataset consists of Asian American artists, with a focus on mixed-race Asian Americans as an exponentially increasing ethnic group in the U. S. (Census, 2020). Mixed-race individuals have an added layer of societal multiformity that presents a unique opportunity to examine how they might explore their lived experience navigating multiple cultural frameworks through the creation of art. By limiting this dataset to second and third generation immigrants, we also examine this ethnicity group’s relationship to the inherited geopolitical histories of the United States relating to their involvement in 20th century conflict across countries in East and Southeast Asia. The focus on abstraction presents the opportunity for a more subjective, illustrative process with its resistance to direct categorization and fixed interpretation. Through examining the tension between lived experience as described by museums and artists and visual representation, what can we learn about the relationship between artists, art practice, and interpreted meaning? What can a layered reading of museum metadata, artist-authored texts, and visual patterns reveal about how identity operates across artistic practice? Where do institutional and self-authored narratives align or diverge, and how might computational analysis support insights on this topic while considering the limits of this approach, considering the context of a practice of archiving and modern technological application that often flattens complexity?
