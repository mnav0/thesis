# Abstract

The general public experiences art through a vetting process of curation where works are selected to be displayed and contextualized by experts, often organized according to a theme, a time period, or experiences that the artists themselves share. However, new media is continuously being introduced to allow us to experience museum archives online, removing both barriers to entry and allowing for different methods and paths of exploring such archives to extract meaning. This paper introduces a new method of online artistic curation, using machine learning to analyze works of art and identify narratives based on the image data alone. Images are then supplemented with the metadata about the artwork and the artist to expose new ways of thinking about existing collections. This data-first process explores what new meaning can be drawn based on a piece of art’s visual appearance alone, with the goal of extracting new connections and themes surrounding the works and the artists. The approach anchors around abstract art, which is both resistant to fixed interpretation and provides a more emotionally dense entry point into artists’ processing of their own world experiences. The case study dataset focuses on 2nd and 3rd generation immigrant Asian-American artists whose families have been impacted by United States foreign involvement during the 20th century, exploring what conclusions can be drawn about this shared experience through the lens of intergenerational trauma, cultural identity, and the lasting effect of conflict, colonization, and policy. This approach is meant to supplement existing art curation practices with computational analysis rather than replace human methods with machine processing, examining what place technology can have within curatorial practice and the experience of art in the modern age.

# Introduction

In recent years, many museums and cultural institutions have established open access initiatives that publish their collections and data online with the goal of increasing access to art and knowledge (Smithsonian, 2020). The popularity of these initiatives has only increased with the pandemic’s limiting access to museums, leading to a multitude of digital projects that utilize these archives to analyze and expose new ways of experiencing art. These archives allow us to explore specific works outside of the context they may traditionally be presented in as part of an exhibit in a museum, centering the experience in these cases on the work within a new context and considering the data and metadata that make up an individual piece of art. What new meaning can be made by recontextualizing a piece of art using a variety of modern tools and technologies, and how can computational analysis and the visualization of art and artist data support traditional curatorial inquiry using new methods?

Traditional curation is typically centered around the objects rather than the artists who create them. Within this practice, historical narratives and expert summaries are often upheld as “truthful” over what living artists may say about their work in an effort to make the work more universal to the viewer (Kina and Dariotis, 8). When art is curated from underrepresented populations, this practice can become reductive and cause divergence from the artist’s intentions. Through using data from interviews, artist statements, and self-written biographies, a re-centering of the work around the artists themselves can add layers of meaning, especially when we consider art as a reflection of the artist’s experience.

Many artists describe their work as an externalization of self, for example Marc Chagall’s autobiographical claim that “Art seems to me to be a state of soul more than anything else” (1922), but an art world where money drives value can find itself at odds with this methodology. When art markets and curation do focus the narrative on the artist in relation to their work, it is often those whose work represents trauma in a way that the viewer can easily interpret, leaving abstraction behind (Coleman, 1). This trend centers around both increasing access to art and favoring what sells to a larger audience. In an age where open access is redefining who can experience art, how can we explore abstraction as a subset of art that wasn’t necessarily created to portray a concrete image to the audience but rather as a method of processing or a visual encoding created by or for the artist themselves? What can an artist’s body of work reveal about the way they experience the world, their own identity, and their identity in relation to the world around them?

In order to explore and apply this methodology to a bounded dataset, a case study is used to identify a group of artists whose feelings about identity and experiences might be expressed through the work they create. With the goal of identifying common threads between diverse individual experiences that share a unifying factor, the case study dataset consists of Asian-American artists impacted by the brutal actions conducted by the United States in the 20th century in the Philippines, Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia. To place this method within the present day, the dataset focuses on two or three generations removed from direct experience of this conflict and includes work created within the past 20 years. This identity group allows us to explore how the U. S.’s actions have impacted present day generations and their experiences of the world as it relates to cultural history, and how it has led to their current place in society. The dataset is largely centered around mixed-race individuals who might explore the multiplicity of their identity and the tension that comes with it through the creation of art. As one of the largest growing ethnic subgroups in the United States over the past 20 years, focusing on mixed-race Asian-American artists allows for another facet to place the research within present day conversations about race and identity in America (Jones 2024).

Through exploring what art and artist data and metadata can reveal when placed in new context or organized in different ways, we examine curatorial thinking about identity and experience as it relates to abstract art. Rather than reducing artists’ experiences to what their art reveals, we combine this with testimonials in their voices, considering what similarities might appear across works of art created by those who might have been impacted by a strong geopolitical and military force like the United States and exploring what the lasting effects of that force might be for individuals currently living in America.
